AICA Arabic-First Multimodal RAG Strategy for
Saudi Sovereign Enterprise Deployment
Executive Summary
AICA  should  treat  Arabic-first  Multimodal  RAG  as  a  core  architectural  requirement,  not  an
enhancement. In Saudi enterprise and government environments, a large share of high-value “knowledge”
is  not  clean,  predominantly-English  HTML.  It  is  Arabic  PDFs,  scanned  circulars,  forms,  image-heavy
reports, screenshots, and bilingual artifacts. Under these conditions, text-only RAG is structurally blind
to key evidence: page layout, tables, stamps/signatures, embedded figures, and the “visual semantics” that
often carry the meaning. Research explicitly shows that text-only RAG cannot exploit visual cues in real-
world multimodality documents, motivating vision-language (VLM)-based RAG pipelines. 
AICA’s  defensible  competitive  position  in  Saudi  Arabia  is  therefore:  sovereign  Arabic-first  grounded
answering  with  audit-grade  multimodal  evidence—including  page/region-level  citations,  attached
media,  and  permission-aware  retrieval  that  is  compatible  with  PDPL and  national  cybersecurity
expectations (notably the  NCA ECC/CCC control families). PDPL applies broadly to processing of personal
data in the Kingdom and, in some scenarios, processing of personal data of individuals residing in the
Kingdom  by  parties  outside  the  Kingdom,  and  it  defines  “processing”  very  broadly  (including  storing,
retrieving,  disclosing,  and  transmitting).   The  NCA’s  ECC  and  CCC  set  baseline  cybersecurity
requirements for national entities (and encourage broader adoption), including expectations for continuous
compliance, logging/monitoring, incident management, cryptography, and cloud/hosting controls. 
Strategic position (opinionated):
Multimodal RAG is critical for AICA’s credibility in Saudi regulated and semi-regulated sectors
because the most defensible answers require evidence objects (page snippets, screenshots, tables,
forms, audio timestamp clips), not just paraphrased text; multimodal retrieval materially reduces
“Arabic OCR brittleness” and “layout loss.” 
AICA should adopt a dual-layer retrieval architecture: (1) best-in-class Arabic text retrieval (hybrid
lexical+dense, Arabic-aware normalization, Arabic reranking), and (2) vision-first page retrieval for
visually rich and scanned documents (e.g., ColPali-like page embeddings) with explicit linkage to
OCR/layout outputs. This is more robust than “OCR-only + text embeddings,” which fails on low-
quality scans and layout-dependent meaning. 
For Saudi sovereign hosting, AICA should prioritize self-hostable components for search,
orchestration, evaluation, and monitoring, and treat vendor components as swappable modules to
avoid lock-in. (Examples of self-hostable search foundations include Elastic self-managed
deployments; open-source vector+hybrid approaches exist in OpenSearch.)

Key recommendations (practical):
Build an Arabic-first Multimodal Document Representation (“document object model”) that
preserves page layout, bounding boxes, reading order , tables, figures, and links every extracted
claim back to a stable evidence anchor (doc_id, page, bbox / timestamp). The goal is “audit-grade
grounded answers,” not just “chat.” 
Use hybrid retrieval (BM25 + dense vectors) for Arabic text plus VLM page retrieval for scanned/
layout-heavy PDFs, then fuse results with a deterministic rank-fusion layer and an Arabic reranker . 
Treat Arabic as a first-class subsystem: Arabic OCR quality controls, Arabic normalization and
morphology-aware query expansion, Arabic embeddings and reranking, Arabic citation UX, and
bilingual Arabic↔English bridging that works even when documents are mixed-language. (This is
where “supports Arabic” typically collapses into “Arabic works poorly.”) 
Build governance to match Saudi expectations: PDPL-aware data minimization and storage controls;
lifecycle transparency, privacy/security-by-design and decision traceability aligned with Saudi Data
and Artificial Intelligence Authority (SDAIA) AI Ethics Principles; cybersecurity logging, incident
response, encryption/key management aligned to National Cybersecurity Authority (NCA) ECC/
CCC. 
Research Scope and Method
This research focused on Arabic-first multimodal retrieval + enterprise assistant delivery under Saudi
sovereignty constraints rather than generic RAG templates.
Research approach
Multimodal RAG and document AI: prioritizing recent peer-reviewed research and widely cited
preprints on VLM-based retrieval, visually rich document benchmarks, and OCR-free / layout-aware
document understanding (e.g., ColPali, VisRAG, LayoutLMv3, Donut, DocVQA, DocLayNet). 
Arabic-first components: Arabic OCR limitations and failure modes; Arabic embeddings and Arabic
language model foundations (e.g., AraBERT; multilingual embeddings). 
Saudi policy and controls: PDPL guidance and national cybersecurity control frameworks (ECC/CCC)
and Saudi Cloud First policy statements relevant to data localization expectations. 
Competitive intelligence: competitor documentation for language support, grounding, connectors/
permissions, and deployment models, with special attention to Arabic limitations and sovereign
hosting feasibility. 
Source categories (priority order) 1) Official documentation and standards-like government publications
(PDPL guidance, NCA ECC/CCC, Cloud First policy). 
2) Research papers and benchmarks (ArXiv, ACL/NAACL, OpenReview, CVF). 
3)  Vendor  technical  docs  (language  support,  grounding  behavior ,  connectors/permissions,  deployment
models).

Limitations and uncertainty
“Arabic support quality” is rarely measured publicly as Arabic-first enterprise usability, so
competitor comparisons often require separating documented feature support (high confidence)
from performance/quality claims (lower confidence) unless benchmarked. 
Some Saudi government guideline PDFs were not directly retrievable from the primary domain due
to access controls; where possible, this report relies on accessible official alternatives (e.g., PDPL
guidance PDF and AI ethics principles PDF). 
Multimodal RAG remains fast-moving; the architecture recommendations emphasize modularity so
AICA can swap retrievers, OCR engines, and VLMs without redesigning the whole product. 
Observed Facts About Multimodal RAG
This section is facts-only (no recommendations), grounded in cited sources.
Multimodal RAG exists because text-only RAG is insufficient for real documents
VisRAG explicitly motivates multimodal RAG by stating that current RAG systems are “solely based on
text,” making it impossible to utilize vision information like layout and images that are crucial in
real-world multi-modality documents; VisRAG proposes a VLM-based RAG pipeline to address this. 
ColPali introduces a VLM trained to produce multi-vector embeddings from images of document
pages, paired with late-interaction matching; it reports outperforming “modern document retrieval
pipelines” while being “drastically simpler , faster and end-to-end trainable.” 
Donut (OCR-free Document Understanding Transformer) argues OCR-dependent pipelines suffer
from OCR cost, inflexibility across languages/document types, and OCR error propagation; it
proposes an OCR-free VDU approach and reports strong performance on document understanding
tasks. 
Layout-aware document understanding is an established research direction
LayoutLMv3 is a multimodal Transformer for Document AI with unified text+image masking and
word-patch alignment to learn cross-modal alignment, targeting tasks including form
understanding, receipt understanding, and document visual question answering. 
DocLayNet is a large human-annotated layout segmentation dataset (80,863 pages) designed for
diverse corporate document layouts and 11 layout classes, enabling evaluation/training for layout
analysis. 
DocVQA provides a benchmark dataset for question answering on document images (tens of
thousands of QA pairs), supporting evaluation of document-image understanding. 
Arabic OCR is intrinsically challenging and quality varies
A review of Arabic OCR highlights Arabic script properties that complicate recognition (including
cursive writing, dots/diacritics, right-to-left direction, and morphological richness).

A comparative OCR study (including Tesseract, Amazon Textract, and Google Document AI) explicitly

Public vendor documentation shows uneven Arabic OCR support: Google Cloud Vision OCR lists

Arabic NLP foundations exist, but Arabic-first quality is not automatic
AraBERT is a transformer-based model pre-trained specifically for Arabic; the paper characterizes
Arabic as morphologically rich and reports strong performance across Arabic NLP tasks compared to
baselines. 
Multilingual E5 text embedding models are open-source and evaluated in a technical report;
documentation also cautions that low-resource languages may see performance degradation. 
Arabic speech recognition exists, but must handle dialects and code-switching
Whisper is trained on 680,000 hours of multilingual/multitask supervised data and supports
multilingual speech recognition; external evaluation work notes Whisper demonstrates strong
capabilities across multiple languages including Arabic (though Arabic labeled hours are limited
relative to total training). 
Saudi governance and cybersecurity controls are concrete and operationally relevant
PDPL guidance states PDPL applies to processing of personal data in the Kingdom and, in certain
cases, processing of personal data of individuals residing in the Kingdom by parties outside the
Kingdom; it defines personal data broadly and defines “processing” to include collecting, storing,
retrieving, using, disclosing, transmitting, publishing, erasing, etc. 
The NCA ECC 2-2024 document states it provides minimum cybersecurity requirements for national
entities within scope, emphasizes continuous compliance, and includes domains and controls
covering cryptography, event logs/monitoring, incident management, backup/recovery, etc. 
The NCA CCC 2-2024 document (as an extension to ECC) defines minimum cybersecurity
requirements for cloud computing for CSPs and CSTs and is explicitly framed in terms of protecting
confidentiality/integrity/availability. 
Saudi Cloud First policy documentation states that all data in Government Cloud and Commercial
Governmental Cloud should be geographically located inside the borders of Saudi Arabia, and
emphasizes cybersecurity evaluation governed by NCA-issued regulations.

Microsoft  documentation states Microsoft 365 Copilot supports Arabic prompts/responses
(language support list includes Arabic). 
Amazon Web Services (AWS) documentation states Amazon Q Business is optimized to respond
in English and only indexes English documents; it recommends indexing only English content. 
Glean  documentation states search results are a subset of documents written in the same
language as the query, and cross-language answering (e.g., English query needing Spanish doc) is

Why Multimodal RAG Matters for AICA
Strategic relevance in Saudi enterprise and government reality
Saudi  organizations  (particularly  in  regulated  and  semi-regulated  environments)  typically  require
auditability, provenance, and defensible evidence. That requirement is incompatible with a retrieval
strategy that treats knowledge as “just text chunks.” When an answer is derived from a scanned circular , a
stamped letter , a table inside a PDF, or a screenshot in a report, the evidence object (page region, extracted
table, diagram) is often the only trustworthy artifact. VisRAG explicitly frames the problem: text-only RAG
cannot use layout/images that matter in real documents. 
Why text-only RAG is insufficient for AICA (especially Arabic-first)
Text-only RAG breaks in specifically Saudi/Arabic contexts due to a compounding set of failure modes:
1)  OCR brittleness in Arabic, especially on low-quality scans, mixed fonts, stamps, rotated pages, and
form-like templates; Arabic OCR is inherently challenging because of script characteristics and diacritics that
can change meaning. 
2) Layout-dependent semantics: many “answers” live in a table cell, a header/footer , a signature block, a
stamp, or a diagram. The meaning is partly visual, not purely lexical. ColPali and VisRAG exist because
modern systems still struggle to use visual cues effectively for document retrieval, motivating VLM-based
retrieval for visually rich documents. 
3) Arabic morphology and business phrasing: Arabic is morphologically rich; enterprises also use code-
switching and loanwords. Arabic-specific models like AraBERT exist because Arabic language understanding
differs materially from English. 
4)  Bilingual  corpora:  Saudi  enterprises  commonly  store  Arabic-first  documents  with  English  technical
annexes, or bilingual policies; naïve “same-language retrieval only” becomes an operational failure (not a
feature gap). Glean’s own documentation effectively acknowledges this limitation by stating cross-language
answering is not GA. 
Inferences for AICA (explicit, not presented as observed facts)
The following are inferences derived from the observed facts and the Saudi product constraints:
If AICA is “Arabic-first,” then Arabic OCR, Arabic retrieval, and Arabic evidence UX must all be
treated as primary KPIs; otherwise, the assistant will appear to “support Arabic” while failing in the
most important Saudi deployments (policies, circulars, and internal directives).

Best Practices for Enterprise AI Conversational Assistants
This section focuses on enterprise-grade practices relevant to Saudi environments and Arabic-first delivery.
Grounding, trustworthiness, and evidence presentation
Enterprise  assistants  must  minimize  hallucination  and  maximize  verifiability.  Google’s  grounding
documentation  describes  grounding  as  connecting  model  output  to  verifiable  data  sources  to  reduce
hallucinations, particularly where accuracy and reliability matter .  In ServiceNow AI Search, Now Assist
explicitly  frames  grounding  by  constraining  the  context  passed  to  the  LLM  to  increase  likelihood  that
responses are grounded in indexed content. 
For AICA, “citations” must go beyond “document title” and support  page and region anchoring (page
number  +  bounding  box)  for  PDFs  and  timestamp  anchoring for  audio/video.  This  aligns  both  with
enterprise audit needs and with Saudi AI ethics expectations that decisions be traceable and explainable.
Permission-aware retrieval and enterprise security controls
Competitors emphasize the importance of permission enforcement at ingestion and query time:
Microsoft 365 Copilot architecture documentation states Copilot uses Microsoft Graph to access user
data in the user’s unique context, including documents the user has permission to access. 
Glean connector documentation emphasizes connectors fetch both content and permissions maps
to ensure results adhere to source permissions. 
For  Saudi  deployments,  permissioning  is  not  just  a  product  feature—it  is  a  compliance  and  trust
requirement. Architecturally, permission checks must be applied before generation and must also control
which evidence objects can be attached or previewed.
Governance, auditability, and alignment to Saudi expectations
Saudi  AI  ethics  guidance  (SDAIA  AI  Ethics  Principles)  explicitly  emphasizes  privacy/security-by-design,
transparency/traceability, and lifecycle governance (plan & design → data preparation → build & validate →
deploy  &  monitor).   NCA  ECC  and  CCC  provide  baseline  cybersecurity  expectations,  including  (as
examples) event logs/monitoring management, incident and threat management, cryptography and key
management,  and  cloud/hosting  cybersecurity  controls.   PDPL  guidance  makes  clear  that  storing,
retrieving, disclosing, and transmitting personal data are all “processing,” which means the assistant’s logs,

Best-practice implication for AICA: governance must cover ingestion pipelines, model prompts, retrieval

Evaluation and observability as production requirements (not optional)
Enterprise-grade assistants require systematic evaluation loops. Ragas is explicitly designed to move from
ad-hoc  “vibe  checks”  to  systematic  evaluation  of  RAG  applications,  and  the  RAGAS  paper  proposes
reference-free evaluation dimensions.   TruLens introduces the “RAG triad” style evaluations (context
relevance, groundedness, answer relevance).  Arize Phoenix is an open-source observability platform for
tracing and evaluation using OpenTelemetry-based instrumentation. 
For AICA specifically, evaluation must be Arabic-first and multimodal-aware:
OCR evaluation must be part of the pipeline because Arabic OCR failures propagate directly into
retrieval failures. 
Multimodal “evidence correctness” must be evaluated: not just whether the answer is correct, but
whether the attached page region/time clip actually contains the claimed evidence.
Arabic-first usability and failure patterns to avoid
AICA must avoid the common trap: “Arabic UI + multilingual embeddings + generic OCR = Arabic product.”
Arabic-first requires:
consistency of terminology and named entities across Modern Standard Arabic and business
phrasing (often with code-switching),
robust handling of Arabic orthographic variation and morphological forms,
evidence UX that feels trustworthy for Arabic readers (clear citations, consistent formatting,
predictable quoting). 
A warning sign in competitor ecosystems is explicit “same-language only” search/answer behavior (e.g.,
Glean’s language support statement), which is directly misaligned with bilingual Saudi corpora.

2)  Enterprise search + RAG platforms with connectors and permission models (Glean, Coveo, Sinequa).
3)  Search infrastructure that organizations self-host for lexical + vector + multimodal retrieval (Elastic,

Bottom line: strong enterprise integration story, but Arabic-first retrieval is structurally blocked unless
customers implement non-native workarounds (translation pipelines, custom ingestion) outside Q Business
itself (inference from English-only indexing statement).
